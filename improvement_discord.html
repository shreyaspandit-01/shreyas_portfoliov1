Problem Definition

Why the Current Discovery Experience Fails

Core Problem Statement
New users who would benefit from Discord communities cannot reliably find them through Discord itself — so they arrive through external channels, land cold, fail to engage, and churn. Communities lose potential members. Discord loses potential power users.

Discord's Server Discovery exists. It's not hidden. But it's essentially a search box and a category filter that returns the largest servers — measured by member count — in any given category. It's the equivalent of YouTube's recommendation algorithm only ever surfacing the most-subscribed channels.

The problem with size-optimised discovery isn't just bad UX. It's structurally harmful. Large servers are noisy, impersonal, and hard to navigate for new members. Smaller, tighter servers — the ones where real conversations happen — are invisible. The platform's own discovery surface is cannibalising its best communities by starving them of members.

From running the Outscal server: In 9 months, I never saw a single member say they found us through Discord Discovery. Every new member came from a LinkedIn post, a WhatsApp group, or word of mouth. Discord was our communication layer, not our growth channel. That's a missed opportunity on Discord's part — and a platform problem, not an Outscal problem.

Proposed Solutions

Three Interventions, One Direction

Each of these targets a different stage of the discovery and onboarding funnel. They can be shipped independently. Together, they form a coherent improvement to the new-member experience.

01
Interest-First Discovery — Replace Size with Fit
Addresses: Discovery failure at top of funnel

Replace member-count ranking in Server Discovery with a multi-signal relevance score. Signals should include: activity rate (messages per active member per day), response rate (how quickly new messages get replies), retention (what % of joining members are still active 30 days later), and channel-to-member ratio (as a proxy for organisation quality).

Additionally, build a first-time onboarding flow that asks 3 questions — "What are you into?", "What are you trying to do?", "How do you prefer to communicate?" — and uses those answers to surface a curated shortlist of 5-8 servers, not 500. Quality over quantity.

The goal isn't to fix search. It's to make the first 5 minutes feel like you've landed somewhere that was waiting for you.

02
Live Server Snapshot — Show What's Happening Right Now
Addresses: Cold landing experience after joining

When a user joins a server, instead of dropping them in #welcome, show them a "What's Happening Now" card first. Think: the 3 most active threads from the last 2 hours, the last question asked that got multiple replies, and a count of members currently online.

This is a single screen shown once — not a permanent UI change. Its only job is to answer the question every new member has when they join: "Is this place alive, and is there something I can contribute to right now?"

From running Outscal's server, I know the first 30 minutes are everything. If a new member lands and sees activity — a real conversation in progress, a question they can answer — they stay. If they see a list of channels with no hint of what's alive, they leave. This feature closes that gap without requiring server admins to do anything differently.

Primary Metric

Secondary

03
Member-to-Member Introductions — Warm the Cold Start
Addresses: Isolation and early churn after joining

The loneliest moment in Discord is right after you join a large server. Hundreds of members, but nobody knows you're there. This feature changes that with a lightweight mutual interest matching layer: when you join a server, Discord quietly looks at your other servers, your message history, and your stated interests, and identifies 2-3 existing members with overlapping profile data. It then sends both parties a non-intrusive notification: "You and [Name] are both into [interest]. Say hi?"

This is opt-in, privacy-respecting, and requires no action from server admins. It's Discord taking responsibility for the social graph — not just providing the infrastructure but actively facilitating the connections that make communities worthwhile.

The north star metric here is the one that actually matters for long-term retention: does the user have one meaningful conversation in their first week? Our data from Outscal suggested that a single substantive reply — not a "welcome!" from a bot — was the strongest predictor of a member still being active 30 days later.

Honest Assessment

Tradeoffs Worth Talking About

Decision The Gain The Cost
Replacing size-based ranking
Discovery overhaul		 Better fit → higher join retention. Smaller quality servers get visibility. Large servers lose top placement. Some server admins will push back. Gaming the new ranking is a risk.
Interest onboarding (3 questions)
First-time flow		 Dramatically improves first-discovery quality. Sets intent signal for recommendation layer. Adds friction to onboarding. Users who just want to join a specific server won't want to answer questions. Needs a "skip" path.
Member-to-member matching
Social graph feature		 Highest long-term retention impact. Creates genuine human connections. Privacy sensitivity is high. Requires opt-in architecture. Users need to trust Discord with cross-server interest data. Non-trivial trust problem.
Rollout Plan

How I'd Sequence This

Phase 1 — Quick Win
Live Server Snapshot
Lowest technical lift, highest immediate impact on join experience. Ship to 10% of new joiners and measure first-message rate. If it moves, roll out fully. No server admin action required — server-agnostic.
Phase 2 — Core Bet
Interest-First Discovery + Onboarding
Redesign the discovery ranking model and add the 3-question first-time flow. Run as A/B test — new discovery vs. old discovery — measuring join retention at D7 and D30. Accept that large servers will hate this. That's okay.
Phase 3 — Long Bet
Member Matching (Opt-In)
Privacy-first opt-in architecture. Launch to a small cohort of users who explicitly enable "help me find people to talk to." Measure DM initiation rate and long-term retention. If positive, expand opt-in surfaces across the onboarding flow.

Closing Thought

Discord Doesn't Have a Community Problem. It Has a Matchmaking Problem.

The communities exist. The people who'd love them exist. Discord just hasn't built the bridge between them. Every feature I've proposed here is, at its core, a matchmaking feature — connecting the right person to the right community, the right conversation, the right member.

Having built and run a community on Discord, I know that the best moments — the conversations that made people come back every day — were never planned. They happened because two people with overlapping interests happened to be in the same channel at the same time. Discord's job is to make that "happen to" a lot less accidental.

The north star: A new Discord user who fills in 3 interest fields should be able to find a community where they have a real conversation within their first 30 minutes. That's not a high bar. It's just not something Discord currently delivers. These three features, shipped in sequence, get there.
