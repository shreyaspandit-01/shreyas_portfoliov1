LinkedIn has 1 billion users. Its feed surfaces content from people you barely know. Its job search buries signals under noise. Its notifications are mostly irrelevant. Here's how to fix the parts that matter most.
Feed Quality Job Discovery Notifications
The Problem
A Network of Connections. A Feed of Strangers.

LinkedIn's core promise is professional networking. But the product has drifted so far from that promise that most users now experience it as a content platform — one where the algorithm serves posts from people they've never met, skipping over updates from people they actually know.

The feed has been optimised for engagement metrics: likes, comments, shares. That optimisation produced a platform full of inspirational posts, humble brags, and performative vulnerability from accounts with large followings — and buried the actual professional signal that makes LinkedIn valuable.

The algorithm learned to serve viral. It forgot to serve useful.

The job search product has a parallel problem. A search for "Product Manager, Delhi" returns hundreds of listings — many of which are weeks old, many of which have already been filled, and many of which the user is wildly under or overqualified for. There is very little filtering intelligence. The burden of qualification sits entirely with the user.

Analysis
Where It Breaks
Three Specific Failures Worth Fixing
Not everything about LinkedIn is broken. The professional identity layer, the messaging infrastructure, the company pages — these work. What follows are the three specific surfaces where the product actively makes the experience worse.
Critical
The Feed Serves Reach, Not Relevance
Posts from people with large follower counts surface ahead of posts from first-degree connections. A post from someone you've never met gets more visibility than an update from your actual colleague. The network effect is inverted.
Critical
Job Listings Have No Freshness Signal
A job posted 45 days ago sits alongside one posted today. Many listings are "evergreen" — companies keep them live even after filling the role. Users apply to ghost jobs with no way of knowing. Rejection or silence follows. Trust erodes.
Significant
Notifications Are Noise, Not Intelligence
"X viewed your profile." "Congratulate Y on their work anniversary." "Your post is getting traction." Almost none of these notifications require action. They create a pavlovian check-in loop without delivering value. The ones that matter — a recruiter message, a relevant job alert — get buried.
The Core Tension
LinkedIn makes money from advertising and recruiter subscriptions. Both are served by high engagement — more time on platform, more content interaction, more notifications clicked. The product incentives point toward a noisy, high-engagement feed. The user's interest points toward a quiet, high-signal one. These are in direct conflict, and right now the platform's interests are winning.
Context
By the Numbers
The Scale of the Problem

Numbers that contextualise why getting this right matters:

Feed
~40% of LinkedIn users say their feed shows them content they find irrelevant. (Source: user surveys, LinkedIn transparency reports)
Job Search
~30% of job listings on LinkedIn are estimated to be filled or expired at the time of application. Users have no way to tell.
Notifications
LinkedIn sends an average of 12-18 push notifications/week per active user. Open rates have declined steadily as content quality has dropped.
The Opportunity
LinkedIn has more professional context per user than any other platform. Skills, experience, connections, job history, content consumed. It uses a fraction of that data to personalise the experience. That gap is the product opportunity.
The Improvements
Three Changes. One Direction.

Each of these is an independent improvement — they can be shipped and tested separately. But they all point at the same thing: a platform that uses what it knows about you to filter the noise, not amplify it.

1
Feed Mode: Connections First
Feed Algorithm
P1 Priority

Add a persistent toggle — two modes, always visible at the top of the feed. "For You" (current algorithm, reach-weighted, discovery-oriented) and "Connections" (chronological or near-chronological, first and second degree only).

This isn't a new idea — Twitter/X implemented it, Instagram offers it. But LinkedIn hasn't, despite having a stronger rationale for it. The professional context of a first-degree connection update — a job change, a project launch, a career milestone — is almost always more relevant to a user than a viral post from a stranger in the same industry.

The Connections feed should surface: job changes, posts from direct connections, company updates from companies a user's connections work at, and content their connections have commented on (with context). Not likes — comments. A comment requires a formed opinion. A like requires a thumb.

Why This Matters for LinkedIn's Business
Counter-intuitive but true: a feed users trust generates more return visits than a feed they feel manipulated by. Short-term engagement metrics might dip. Long-term daily active users and session depth should improve. The bet is on quality over volume.
Type
Algorithm + UI change
Effort
Medium — toggle UI is simple; feed re-ranking needs work
Primary Metric
Feed session depth (scroll + dwell) for users who switch to Connections mode
Secondary
D30 retention for Connections mode users vs For You
Risk
Creator ecosystem pushback. Smaller accounts lose reach if users migrate to Connections feed.
2
Job Listings: Freshness & Fit Score
Job Search
P1 Priority

Two changes to job listings, shipped together because each reinforces the other.

Freshness signal: Show a clear age indicator on every listing — not just "Posted 3 weeks ago" but a colour-coded freshness badge. Green: posted in the last 7 days. Amber: 8-21 days. Red: 22+ days, with a warning that the role may have been filled. For roles over 30 days old with no recruiter activity, add a "This listing may be expired" flag. LinkedIn has this data — recruiter logins, listing view counts, application-to-response rates. Surface it.

Fit score: A percentage match displayed on each listing, derived from the user's profile (skills, experience level, location, past roles) against the job description requirements. Not a black box "Easy Apply" badge — an actual breakdown: "You match 7 of 9 required skills. Missing: Python, 3+ years of management experience." This turns job search from a guessing game into an informed decision.

The Trust Angle
Ghost jobs are LinkedIn's biggest trust problem. When users apply to 40 jobs and hear nothing back, they don't blame the companies — they blame the platform. A freshness signal that helps users avoid expired listings directly improves the experience of job searching on LinkedIn, even without changing anything else.
Type
Data surface + UI change
Effort
Medium-high — fit score algorithm, freshness data pipeline
Primary Metric
Application-to-response rate (signal of application quality improving)
Secondary
Time spent on job search per session (should decrease — fewer wasted applications)
Risk
Recruiters may object to freshness flags deprioritising their old listings. Needs careful rollout with recruiter communication.
3
Notification Triage: Signal vs. Noise
Notifications
P2 Priority

Redesign the notification stack around a simple two-tier model: Actions and Updates.

Actions are notifications that require something from the user — a recruiter message, a connection request with a personalised note, a comment that asks them a direct question, a job application status change. These appear prominently, with a push notification, because they represent real professional interactions.

Updates are everything else — profile views, post impressions, work anniversaries, generic "your post did well" alerts. These get batched into a once-daily digest, delivered at a time the user chooses, with no push notification. Just a quiet in-app badge.

The AI layer makes this smarter over time: if a user consistently ignores work anniversary notifications but always opens recruiter messages, the system learns to weight future notifications accordingly. Personalised noise reduction, not one-size-fits-all.

Precedent
Gmail's Priority Inbox (2010) proved that users prefer fewer, smarter notifications to more frequent, dumb ones. LinkedIn has the professional context to do this better than any consumer email product. It just hasn't prioritised it.
Type
Notification architecture + ML layer
Effort
Medium — classification model, digest UI, preference settings
Primary Metric
Notification open rate for Action-tier alerts (target: significant increase vs. current undifferentiated stack)
Secondary
Push notification opt-out rate (target: decrease)
Risk
Reducing notification volume may impact DAU in the short term. Short-term metric dip for long-term trust gain.

LinkedIn Knows More About You Than Almost Any Product. It Should Act Like It.

The frustrating thing about LinkedIn's product gaps isn't that they're hard to fix. It's that the data to fix them already exists. LinkedIn knows your skills, your experience, your connections, your career history, what content you engage with, what jobs you apply for. It uses maybe 20% of that signal to personalise your experience.

The three improvements here are all versions of the same idea: use what you know. A feed that surfaces your actual network. Job listings that tell you whether they're worth applying to. Notifications that respect your attention. None of this requires new data. It requires a product philosophy that prioritises the user's time over the platform's short-term engagement numbers.

That's a harder internal sell than a new feature. But it's the one that builds a product people actually trust — and trust, in professional networking, is the whole game.
