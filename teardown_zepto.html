01 — What Zepto Gets Right

Start with what's genuinely good. Zepto's core product — 10-minute delivery — actually works. Not sometimes, not on good days. It works reliably enough that you stop thinking of it as impressive and start treating it as baseline. That's the highest form of product trust: when a feature becomes invisible because it just works.

The dark store model is the right structural bet. By controlling inventory at the hyperlocal level, Zepto can guarantee availability in a way that Swiggy Instamart and Blinkit can't always match. When you search for something, it's usually in stock. That's logistics as product design.

The 10-minute guarantee isn't just a feature — it's a brand promise that organises every downstream decision. Dark stores, SKU curation, rider density, real-time inventory — all of it exists to protect that one number. When a company aligns itself around one metric this completely, you get genuine product clarity.

The search experience is also better than it gets credit for. Query understanding is solid — search "coke" and you get Coca-Cola variants ranked by purchase frequency, not alphabetically. Checkout is equally clean: cart to payment in under 30 seconds for returning users, with no dark patterns or surprise fees at the final screen.

02 — Where It Starts to Break

Here's what nobody talks about with Zepto: after your 50th order, the product feels exactly the same as it did on your 5th. There is no sense of the app knowing you. No accumulated trust. You are, perpetually, a new user with a purchase history.

The same items get reordered week after week. Zepto has never surfaced a single one proactively. No "your usual" shelf, no "you're running low" nudge, no order template. Every session starts from scratch — the same queries, the same categories, the same choices. This is table-stakes personalisation that every major grocery app has been doing for years.

The home screen problem runs deeper than it looks. Count the surfaces on Zepto's home that exist to serve you versus the ones that serve a brand partner. The space where Zepto could demonstrate it knows you is entirely occupied by paid placement.

The miss isn't that Zepto runs ads — it's that ads are the only thing on the home screen. No personalised shelf, no "based on your last order," no seasonal suggestion tied to purchase history. Every grocery app in a mature market has solved this. Zepto hasn't prioritised it.
03 — The Retention Question

Zepto's current retention strategy: deliver fast, run discounts. That works until Blinkit or Instamart matches delivery times in a user's locality — and in many areas, they already have. At that point, what keeps a user on Zepto?

The answer should be familiarity. A product that has learned your weekly routine. That reminds you when you'd typically reorder something. That gets smarter about your preferences every week. That has basket intelligence — if you buy pasta, it knows to surface parmesan.

Fast delivery solves acquisition. The personalisation layer solves long-term retention. Zepto has invested everything in the former and almost nothing in the latter. That's a strategic gap, not an oversight.
04 — Three Changes Worth Making
01 — Smart Basket High Impact
Repeat Order Intelligence
A persistent shelf on the home screen, pre-populated based on purchase frequency and recency. Not "order again" — smarter. If milk gets bought every 5 days and it's been 4, surface it. If eggs always follow bread, bundle the suggestion. The data exists. The product surface doesn't.
Metric — Orders per user per month. Target: 6-8 toward 12+ for habitual users.
02 — Substitution Transparency High Impact
Pre-approve or Cancel
When an item is out of stock after ordering, Zepto currently substitutes silently. The fix: show the proposed swap before the rider leaves the dark store. Give the user 90 seconds to approve, reject, or remove. This eliminates the most common complaint about Zepto across every review forum that exists.
Metric — Post-delivery negative ratings and refund request rate.
03 — Discovery Mode Medium + Long-term
Separate Exploration from Shopping
A distinct tab that is explicitly not a shopping surface — no add-to-cart, no price prominence. Recipe-based discovery, seasonal highlights, editorial content about what's fresh. Let users browse without buying pressure. Purchase intent follows naturally. It also creates a content moat that competitors can't copy with logistics alone.
Metric — Session length and basket size for users who engage with discovery content.
Bottom Line
Zepto solved the hard problem first. The delivery infrastructure is real. Now the product layer needs to catch up.
